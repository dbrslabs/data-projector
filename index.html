Mapping the Guardian

Machine learning, a subfield of computer science, is good at pattern recognition, classification, and predictive analytics. Machine learning algorithms can learn from sample data sets and apply that learning to data that it has never before encountered.

Guardian Galaxy is a visualization of all of the articles from The Guardian from 1921 to present, arranged by relationship. The more related two articles are, the closer their placement. In other words, if you were to draw a line between two articles, that lineâ€™s length corresponds to how related those two articles are. Visualizing The Guardian archives allows us to explore articles in a way that favors discovery over directed search and emphasizes broader relationships and trends.

How does the relationship engine work?

Using the doc2vec algorithm, each article is assigned values for hundreds of parameters. These values are determined by an unsupervised learning pass. This means that the number assigned to a parameter is inferred alorithmically based on its properties and context.

The result is that articles share similar values with similar articles. But how can we represent these similarities without having to compare hundreds of numbers between each article we want to compare?

The t-SNE algorithm converts high-dimensional data to low-dimensional data while retaining as much information as possible. Think of the hundreds of parameters like coordinates in hundred+ dimensional space (like latitude and longitude but with way more numbers). Pretty hard to imagine right? By flattening hundreds dimensions to just three, we can now represent that relationship between articles in a way that is easily understandable!

GUARDIAN GALAXY is a visualization of articles from the Science section of The Guardian from 1921-2016. Using the machine learning tools word2vec, t-SNE, and k-means, articles are grouped into clusters based on semantic relationship. Each point represents an article.

Learn more about GUARDIAN GALAXY.

Each circle below represents a group of related articles, or â€œcluster". Machine Learning algorithms convert the words of each article into numerical vector-based representations. Articles with similar vectors are closer to one another than articles with dissimilar vectors. The t-SNE algorithm then converts those coordinates from high-dimensional space (think latitude and longitude, but with way more numbers) to three dimensional space.

To see all or none of the clusters, click on the grey button or left-most button.

The article list on the right displays a random sampling from the selected clusters. The circles beside each headline represent the cluster to which each article belongs. Click on the headline to read a snippet from the article. At the bottom of the snippet are the Top Five Related Articles as determined by the machine learning tools. Each time you move back from an article to the main listing, a new article list from the selected clusters is generated.
